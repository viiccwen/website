For many CS students, the fastest path to an internship usually looks the same: grind LeetCode, submit resumes, and build side projects. And after sending out at least 50 applications, **what often comes back is just a string of rejection emails**. That has practically become part of the standard computer science experience.

<img src="https://media1.tenor.com/m/zA1Va-a2z8MAAAAd/unemployed-final.gif" width="40%" style="display: block; margin: 0 auto;" />

But as the title suggests, I ended up taking a very different route. It was rougher, less predictable, and definitely not the mainstream choice. Instead of putting most of my time into interview prep, I chose to invest in open-source projects and technical communities. Starting as a contributor nobody knew, I slowly built up experience, credibility, and impact. In the end, that path led me to a SWE Intern offer from Taiwan Mobile.

## Where It Started

At that time, I was trying everything most students try: keeping my GPA up, building side projects, learning relevant technologies, and even joining communities and speaking at events. But the reality was simple: after sending application after application, I was still getting mostly rejections. It was frustrating. You feel like you are doing a lot, but none of it seems to turn into actual opportunities.

My path into open source started during a previous internship, where I ran into a bug and thought I would do what most people do: open an issue and wait for someone else to respond. But then I became curious. Why did the bug happen in the first place? So I started reading the source code, used AI to help me narrow down the cause, and eventually submitted a pull request that got merged.

That was the first time I truly felt that I was not just someone using technology. I could actually participate in it, and maybe even leave a small mark on the world. That experience made me rethink everything. **Maybe I did not have to follow the usual path after all.**

## Going Deeper with Apache Mahout

Although I had started contributing to open source, I did not have much time during the first semester of my junior year, so my activity slowed down. The real turning point came when my friend Jay introduced me to [Apache Mahout](https://github.com/apache/mahout).

<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/10/Apache_Mahout_Logo.png" width="50%" style="display: block; margin: 0 auto;" />

At first, it was not easy at all. Compared with the projects I had worked on before, Mahout had evolved into an area focused on quantum machine learning and GPU parallel computation, which was far outside my previous experience. I had to spend much more time understanding the background knowledge, the architecture, the mailing list discussions, and the community context. Sometimes, even clarifying a single issue required repeated research and experiments.

But that process taught me a lot. It was also when I began to engage with engineering as something deeper than just writing code. I learned how to think about maintainable software, how to collaborate with developers from different backgrounds, and how to make code understandable and acceptable to others.

Over time, I became able to contribute more consistently, from small fixes to feature improvements, and I also began reviewing other people's pull requests. Up to now, I have accumulated [30 PRs](https://github.com/apache/mahout/pulls?q=is%3Apr+author%3Aviiccwen+) and [60 code reviews](https://github.com/apache/mahout/pulls?q=is%3Apr+reviewed-by%3Aviiccwen). Behind those numbers are countless rounds of discussion, challenges, revisions, and learning.

## Community Opened the Door

Beyond technical growth, open source also helped me build connections with more communities. I joined [Open Source 4 You - ALC Taipei](https://github.com/opensource4you), where I met many impressive contributors, committers, and PMC members from major international projects. To me, it felt like stepping into a completely new world. People I had previously only seen on GitHub or in documentation were suddenly right there in front of me, and they were all incredibly willing to share their experience.

I also started attending their open source gatherings, which were sometimes hosted at the Taiwan Mobile office. Through one of those events, I met Marie, who works in Taiwan Mobile's CIO Office and also serves as the Deputy Secretary General of IMA. At first, it was just a casual conversation. I shared my background, what I had been working on, and the fact that I was looking for an internship.

<img src="/posts/from-opensource-to-twm-intern/taiwan-mobile-office.webp" width="45%" style="display: block; margin: 0 auto;" />

What surprised me was how quickly things moved from there. After learning about my open source experience and how much time I had invested in it, Marie helped arrange the next interview steps. The process had its twists and turns, but in the end, it worked out well.

> The experience and effort I had built up through open source were finally turning into a real opportunity.

## Looking Back

When I reflect on this journey, I do not think there was one single decision that made everything work. It was the accumulation of several important choices.

First, I did not treat open source as just an extra bonus on my resume. I treated it as one of my main directions. That meant I was not just fixing a random bug once in a while. I **kept reading code, joining discussions, and contributing regularly** until I became part of the project.

Second, I focused on systems and problems themselves, instead of only chasing closed issues. Whether I was fixing a bug, designing a feature, or participating in a review, I **tried to understand the reasoning and trade-offs behind the work**. That pushed me beyond simply writing correct code and toward making real engineering decisions in real environments.

Finally, I learned the importance of **making your work visible**. Open source is public by nature. Your code, discussions, and contribution history become long-term proof of what you can do. When opportunities appear, you do not need to package everything from scratch because the evidence is already there.

## A Few Thoughts for Anyone Still Searching

If you are also looking for an internship, or if you feel lost about what direction to take, here are a few thoughts.

First, you do not have to follow the most mainstream path. LeetCode and side projects absolutely matter, but they are not the only way to grow. If you are stubborn enough and willing to endure a harder path, open source can give you a very different kind of development, especially if you want to work on real systems and real problems.

Second, start small, but stay consistent. Your first contribution might only be a documentation fix or a minor bug. What matters is not how big it is, but whether you are willing to keep going and gradually move toward more important work.

Third, learn how to read and understand systems built by other people. Many people can write code quickly, and AI can now generate a lot of code in minutes, but that does not automatically mean someone can understand the design and evolution of a large project. Open source trains that skill constantly, and companies care about it.

Finally, participate in communities. Whether it is online discussion, community meetups, or technical talks, those are all important ways to become visible and build connections. A lot of opportunities do not come from cold applications. They grow naturally from interaction.

> Open source did not just give me technical skills. It gave me a way to participate in the world. You are no longer only a user of tools. You can contribute, influence, and maybe even change something.
